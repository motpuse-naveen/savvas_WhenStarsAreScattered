Author’s Craft

Writing in Two Languages

Juan Felipe Herrera is a bilingual poet and writer and was the first Latino poet laureate of the United States (2015-17). His memoir, Calling the Doves / El canto de las palomas, is written in both Spanish and English and has received praise for its readability in both languages.

Hold up the book and point out the title in English and Spanish. Explain to students that this is a bilingual book written in two languages. Then, preview some of the pages to point out the English and Spanish text throughout the book. You can also highlight examples of Spanish words used within the English text. For example, campesinos [kam-puh-see-nohs] for farmworkers and carpa [kar-pah] for tent. Ask students to think about why the memoir is written in two languages and the reasons for using Spanish words within the English text. Use the guiding questions to prompt discussion.

  • Why do you think Herrera wrote his memoir in both Spanish and English? (his memories are in both languages; he wants more people to be able to read the book; he likes writing in both languages)
  • Why do you think some authors use words from another language in their writing? (it makes the writing more realistic; some words can’t be translated; the words might sound better in another language)

Encourage students to compare some of the words, phrases, and lines written in English and Spanish and make a list of cognates (words that have the same meaning in both languages) as they read.

Summary

Following a dedication to the author’s migrant farmworker parents, Herrera begins his memoir with a description of traveling the backroads of California with his parents as they move to a new worksite. On their way to a new site, they would set up camp wherever they stopped—sleeping under a large carpa (tent) and cooking their meals outside over an open fire. One day, Herrera’s father builds a house on top of an abandoned car and the family begins living in the one-room house on wheels. Using poetic language, including similes and metaphors, Herrera describes these experiences in vivid detail, bringing his childhood memories to life.

Summary

The memoir continues with Herrera’s description of the changing seasons. In winter his family trimmed grapevines in Delano. During springtime they traveled to Salinas to pick melons and other fruits and vegetables. Then, back to Delano in summer for pruning, picking, and drying the grapes. As his family drives down the mountains to their new home in the cities of Southern California, Juanito realizes that someday he will follow his own road and let his own voice fly—just like his parents.

Plot Overview

You were born on the road like your father begins the bilingual memoir of Juan Felipe Herrera, the first Latino poet laureate of the United States (2015-17). Born in the “raison capital of the world,” Herrera moved from town to town with his farmworker parents until he was eight years old. Traveling the backroads of California, we learn about the vivid landscapes of his childhood.

Poetic descriptions shed light on his family’s day-to-day experiences, including sleeping under the stars in a large canvas carpa (tent), bathing in a tin bucket, and cooking over the open fire. During lunch breaks, Herrera’s father would make bird calls—specifically, calling the doves by whistling to impersonate their singing. The memoir also describes his parents' vivid storytelling through songs, poems, and animated accounts of their travels and experiences.

When Herrera turns eight years old, his mother decides it is time for their family to settle down so he can go to school. Driving down the mountains toward the cities of Southern California, young Herrera realizes that he, too, has stories to tell and will one day “follow his own road” and "let [his] voice fly"—just like his parents.

Theme Overview

Migrant Farmworkers Juan Felipe Herrera’s farmworker parents are central characters in his childhood memoir. Like many migrant farmworkers, Herrera’s family experienced difficulty and hardship due to the grueling nature of the work and the ongoing challenges of being transient. At the same time, they developed deep and loving connections with each other and their fellow farmworkers. Throughout the memoir, we see how Herrera’s family loves and cares for each other—creating a sense of "home" and community despite being transient.

Storytelling The memoir also explores the significant role of storytelling in Herrera's family. His mother told stories through songs and poetry, while his father played the harmonica and retold anecdotes from his travels to "el Norte" from Chihuahua, Mexico, as a teenager. Through these stories, Herrera learns about his family roots and gains a deeper understanding of his Mexican heritage. He also develops an appreciation and love for language, poetry, and the art of storytelling—contributing to the development of his own poetic voice and storytelling techniques.

Activity Support

Figurative Language

Simile & Metaphor

Use the common examples listed below to demonstrate the difference between similes and metaphors (similes use the words “like” or “as”; metaphors do not). Point out that both similes and metaphors compare things that have similar qualities.

Examples:

Similes* Metaphors
  • Her heart is like gold.
  • His smile is as bright as the sun.
  • She has a heart of gold.
  • He is my sunshine.

Display and read the examples aloud and have students identify the comparisons in each example. Explain that similes and metaphors are considered figurative language because the meaning is not literal (her heart is not really gold; his smile is not really the sun). They are compared because they share similar qualities (her heart is precious like gold; his smile is bright like the sun).

Use the guiding questions and sentence frames to prompt and support discussion:

  • What is “her heart” compared to? Why?
  • What is “his smile” compared to? Why?

Sentence Frames:

Her heart is compared to blank line────────── because blank line────────── .

His smile reminds the writer of blank line────────── because blank line────────── .

Explain that authors use similes and metaphors to make their writing more descriptive and that there are many examples in Calling the Doves. Tell students to look for similes and metaphors while they read and to think about how they help to describe specific details.

*You may want to point out that the words simile and similar share the same Latin root simil, which means “like” or “same.” Also, the word similar is a Spanish-English cognate—it has the same meaning in Spanish and English.

Activity Support

Unlock a Key Sentence

Display and read aloud the key sentence. Then guide students in unlocking its meaning by discussing each sentence chunk using the protocol described below.

Start with the sentence chunk that tells who or what the sentence is mostly about. Continue to unpack meaning and connect ideas, chunk by chunk, using the guiding questions in the table below.

In their bright colors, | campesinos | dotted the land | like tropical birds

Sentence Chunk Discuss Meaning
campesinos

-Who/what is the sentence mostly about? (the campesinos—farmworkers)

-Why did the author use the Spanish word for farmworker? Why is it italicized? (to create authenticity / italics indicate it is a Spanish word)
dotted the land

-What does “dotted the land” mean? (to appear at different points on the land)

-Why did the author use the word “dotted”? (from a distance, the farmworkers looked like dots of color)

-What do you think the campesinos are doing? (they might be working in the fields; they could be picking fruits or vegetables; they could be planting crops)
like tropical birds

-What does the word “like” signify? (a comparison; a simile)

-What is being compared? (the campesinos and tropical birds)

-Why did the author choose this comparison? What qualities do they share? (the campesinos clothes are colorful like the feathers of tropical birds; tropical birds migrate from place to place like the campesinos; tropical birds may reflect the places where the campesinos are from)
In their bright colors,

-What does this tell us about the campesinos? (the campesinos wore bright colorful clothes)

-Why does the sentence start with this description? (to introduce the comparison; to bring attention to the bright colors)

Guide students in identifying similes and metaphors found in the text and have them choose one to illustrate. Tell students they can draw the literal or figurative meaning. For example, “My father would pull out a thick green canvas, like a giant tortilla dipped in green tomato sauce” (p. 6) could be drawn as a literal tortilla or as a green canvas tent. Examples of similes and metaphors from the text:

  • - In their bright colors, campesinos dotted the land like tropical birds.
  • - My father would pull out a thick green canvas, like a giant tortilla dipped in green tomato sauce.
  • - From the distance, my house was a short loaf of bread on wheels.
  • - Inside it was a warm cave of conversations.
  • - Our dirt patio was a sand-colored theater where I learned to sing.

Activity Support

Author’s Craft

Display various pages in the book and ask students to point out the Spanish words they notice within the English text. Then, draw students’ attention to the use of Spanish in describing the fiesta in the mountains on page 28. Discuss the use of Spanish words to describe this scene using the following guiding questions:

  • Which words are in Spanish? (fiesta, campesinos, carpa, and churros con canela y azúcar) How do you know they are Spanish words? (they are italicized)
  • Why do you think the author chose to use Spanish for these words? (to give a more realistic/authentic description; the words do not translate well in English; the words sound better in Spanish)
  • What clues does the author provide to help someone who doesn't know Spanish? (the illustrations and context clues: for example, the author references a “circus tent” when describing the “carpa” and the illustration depicts a party/fiesta under a tent—this helps the reader understand that “carpa” means “tent”)
